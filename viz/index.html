Can a walk on the 3D grid avoid every three-in-a-line, forever?

Erdős Problem #193 asks whether an infinite walk with steps from a finite set can visit lattice points with no three ever collinear. Open since 1979. This project attacks it by exact computation — the mathematics driven by an AI (Claude) working under a human engineer’s direction, every result checked by integer arithmetic anyone can re-run. It builds record-breaking walks by repeating one move — enlarge, twist, stitch — at ever-larger scales, and runs a proof programme trying to show that construction can be continued forever.

$M=\begin{pmatrix}3&0&0\\0&0&-3\\0&3&-1\end{pmatrix}$The engine. Every level multiplies the whole walk by M — enlarging it 3× (determinant 27) and twisting it by an angle that never repeats, so no two scales ever line up.

Two equations that carry the project

$$\text{collinear}(x,y,z)\;\Longleftrightarrow\;(y-x)\times(z-x)=\mathbf{0}$$

The rule, exactly. Three grid points line up precisely when this cross product of integer vectors is the zero vector — so every check in the project is exact integer arithmetic, never a floating-point approximation. The record walk passes this test for all 5 quadrillion of its triples.

$$c_k(q,r)\;\le\;C\,r^{\,d},\qquad d=\tfrac{\log\lambda}{\log 3}\approx 1.10\quad(\lambda\approx 3.36)$$

The one thing left to prove. This is Lemma R — crowding regularity. It says any ball of radius $r$ holds at most about $r^{d}$ walk points ($d\approx1.10$): the walk threads space like a nearly one-dimensional curve, never bunching into a blob. And a stitch’s number of legal moves falls off cleanly with local crowding $c$ — measured $\log(\text{legal words})\approx 11.9-0.265\,c$ — so bounded crowding means a stitch can never run out of options, and the construction runs forever. Every other step is proven or a finite check; this single bound is the whole remaining gap.

Origin story

This project got unstuck because of a dishwasher. Every early attempt built self-copying patterns that stacked like plates — each scale aligned with the last, and alignment is exactly what this problem punishes (we eventually proved all such aligned constructions must fail — the Perron–Frobenius no-go in the research log). Then Amanie shared a Reddit post about loading bowls in a dishwasher, and Erik noticed the bowls do something plates don’t: they nest at a tilt — the same shape repeated, each one offset and rotated from the last. Translated into mathematics, that became the whole construction: enlarge the walk by a matrix that also twists it by an angle that never repeats, so no two scales can ever line up. That one idea — imbrication — is the backbone of the record walks and of the entire proof strategy on this site.

The project is built and run by Erik Kalviainen (ekalvi), a software engineer in Canada, on an always-on Mac in his homelab. The mathematics, code and computations are driven by Claude Fable 5 (Anthropic’s research-grade AI) working autonomously under Erik’s direction, with strategy review from a second AI (GPT 5.6) — and every result is verified by exact integer arithmetic that anyone can re-run. Idea credit where it’s due: to Amanie, and to whoever loaded that dishwasher.
