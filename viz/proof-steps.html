The proof, end to end

From the concept, to the construction, to an interactive demo you can try to break, to the single lemma still standing between it and a full proof. Every number here is a real, exhaustively computed value from the project (github.com/ekalvi/erdos-193).

1 ·The concept: three properties, pick two

A curve of steps can be a smooth thread (dimension 1, not a crinkly fractal), it can be triple-free (no three points ever in a line), and it can use a finite menu of steps (bounded hops, as Erdős #193 demands). You can always get two — but the corner where all three meet appears to be empty. That empty corner is why the problem is hard, and it's why our object has to be a fractal thread.

How to read it. Each corner is a property you'd want; a curve that achieves two of them lives on the edge between those corners and is pushed off the third. Erdős #193 requires the bottom two (triple-free + finite menu) and doesn't even ask for dimension 1 — it just asks whether such a curve can exist at all. Our construction is the flag on that bottom vertex.

In 2D the plane is provably too cramped for this to continue (the level-2 stitches jam) — that's Gerver–Ramsey. In 3D, the identical machine with the 124-move menu ran eight rounds to a certified 311,738-point walk, and the open question is whether it provably runs forever.

6 ·The one thing that can go wrong: crowding

Because everything already placed is safe forever (step 4), the construction can only fail one way: a stitch gets stuck — a gap where every legal hop would make three points line up. That happens only if too many earlier points are crowded around the gap, throwing off too many forbidden lines. So the whole question of "does it run forever?" collapses to one geometric fact about the shape:

Lemma R is exactly that statement: no matter how large the walk grows or where you look, any small ball contains at most about radius-many points (radius^1.10) — the walk is a fractal thread, never a blob, forever. Everything else in the proof is done; this one lemma is the whole remaining game, and it's the same claim as "our walk really sits on that bottom vertex of the triangle in step 1."

7 ·Where it stands

  • Base, inheritance, induction — a triple-free seed exists (certified to 311,738 points); enlargement freezes it safe forever; the levels chain by plain induction. Proven.
  • No blow-up — the enlarged anchors are provably ≥3 apart at every scale, so crowding can never run away to a blob. Closed by an exhaustive check over all 78,999,838 stitch words. Proven (a loose, but real, bound).
  • Exact self-similarity — in the right metric the enlarging matrix is exactly a 3× rotation, so the walk is a genuine self-similar fractal (a clean integer identity). Proven.
  • No far-tangle — a "spool" coordinate proves the thread can never drift back near far-away parts of itself. Proven.
  • Lemma R (the tight bound) — that the thread is uniformly lean at every scale. In its sharpest form this is a single textbook condition — the Open Set Condition for the self-similar system (the scaled copies tile cleanly instead of piling up). It is measured true across the whole 311,738-point walk, and proving it is the sole remaining step.

In short: Erdős #193 holds — yes — modulo one geometric regularity lemma, with every other piece proven or certified by exhaustive computation. The full, referee-checkable statement lives in CONDITIONAL-THEOREM.md; the live hunt for that last lemma is on the research log.
